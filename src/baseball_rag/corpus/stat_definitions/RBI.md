Runs Batted In (RBI) credits a batter for each runner who scores as a direct result of their at-bat, with some exceptions such as when an error precedes the score or when the batter hits into a double play. The statistic was officially adopted by Major League Baseball in 1920 and has since become one of the most referenced measures of run production, despite ongoing debates about its merit. RBIs accumulate throughout games, seasons, and careers, with career totals often cited as a testament to a player's ability to perform in clutch situations.

The logic behind RBI as a metric is intuitive: baseball ultimately revolves around scoring runs, so any player who consistently drives in runs provides clear value. However, modern analysts have identified significant limitations with the statistic. RBIs are heavily dependent on opportunity—a player batting third or fourth in a lineup gets more chances to bat with runners on base than a leadoff hitter. Additionally, a player's RBI total is influenced by how well their teammates hit ahead of them and reach base when they come to the plate.

Hall of Famer Hank Aaron holds the all-time career RBI record with 2,297, followed closely by Babe Ruth's 2,214. Among the criticisms lodged at RBIs is that they reward players for being in the right spot in the lineup rather than necessarily being the most skilled hitters. A player can have an extraordinary RBI season partly because they had exceptional protection around them in the batting order, while a great hitter on a weak team may see their RBI totals suppressed by lack of opportunities.
