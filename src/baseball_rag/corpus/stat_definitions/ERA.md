Earned Run Average measures a pitcher's effectiveness at preventing runs by calculating how many earned runs they surrender per nine innings pitched. The formula is straightforward: (Earned Runs × 9) / Innings Pitched. An ERA of 3.50 means the pitcher would allow an average of 3.5 runs if they pitched nine full innings. Since its creation in the early 1900s, it has become the most widely recognized metric for evaluating pitching performance and remains central to discussions about Cy Young Award voting and Hall of Fame cases.

ERA's enduring dominance is partly due to its intuitive nature and historical continuity rather than its accuracy as a complete performance measure. The statistic does not account for factors within a pitcher's control versus those influenced by defense, ballpark effects, or luck on balls in play. A pitcher who induces weak contact that fielders convert into outs will have a lower ERA than an identical pitcher with poor defensive backing, even if their actual abilities are the same. This limitation has driven interest in newer metrics like FIP and xFIP that attempt to isolate pitching performance.

The lowest single-season ERAs in baseball history include Tim Keefe's 0.86 in 1880 and addie Joss's 1.16 in 1908, though these occurred in radically different run environments than modern baseball. In the pitcher's era of recent decades, sub-2.00 ERAs have become vanishingly rare as offense crept upward due to ballpark dimensions and strategic changes. Clayton Kershaw's 1.69 ERA from 2013 stands out in modern context, demonstrating how difficult it has become to dominate to that degree over a full season.
