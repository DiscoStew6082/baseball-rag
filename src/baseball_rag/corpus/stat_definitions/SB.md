A stolen base is awarded when a runner advances one base without the ball being hit, typically by sprinting from their current base to the next while the pitcher delivers to home plate. The statistic tracks how many bases a player successfully steals during a game, season, or career. It represents one of baseball's most exciting elements—the tension of a close play at second base when a speed threat attempts to steal has captivated fans for over a century.

The risk-reward calculation inherent in stolen bases makes it a fascinating metric to analyze. Each successful steal increases run expectancy by advancing a runner into scoring position, but each caught stealing kills an offense's momentum and costs the team an out that could have been used for something else. The break-even point historically has been estimated around 70-75% success rate, below which aggressive base-stealing becomes counterproductive to winning baseball. Modern analytics has refined this understanding further using context-specific run expectancy models.

Rickey Henderson owns the career stolen base record by a staggering margin, swiping 1,406 bases during his legendary career—more than 400 ahead of second-place Lou Brock. His combination of speed and patience made him uniquely suited to exploit the steal, as he could wait for optimal conditions rather than forcing attempts. The modern era has seen increased sophistication in base-stealing strategy, with teams using shift timing, pitcher step-off patterns, and launch angle correlations to determine when stealing offers positive expected value.
