Batting average is one of the oldest and most recognized statistics in baseball, calculated by dividing a player's total number of hits by their total at-bats. The formula is straightforward: AVG = Hits / At-Bats. A player who records 150 hits in 500 at-bats would finish with a .300 batting average, which has long been considered the benchmark for productive hitters. The number is expressed as a decimal, typically written with three digits after the decimal point (e.g., .300 rather than .3000).

The single-season batting average record is famously held by Nap Lajoie, who hit .426 in 1901, though some argue that Hugh Duffy's .440 in 1894 deserves consideration given the different run environment of the era. The last player to bat .400 for a full season was Ted Williams, who finished at .406 in 1941—a feat many baseball historians consider the greatest achievement in hitting history. No player has come within 30 points of .400 since, with modern pitching specialization and specialized bullpens making sustained excellence at that level nearly impossible.

While batting average remains culturally significant and widely referenced, modern analysts recognize its limitations as a complete measure of offensive value. The stat treats all hits equally—a bunt single counts the same as a line-drive double—and it completely ignores walks and other ways players reach base. This is why on-base percentage (OBP) has gained prominence in contemporary baseball analysis, as it captures more fully how often a player contributes to scoring opportunities.
