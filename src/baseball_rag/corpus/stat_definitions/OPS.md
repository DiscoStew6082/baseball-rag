On-Base Plus Slugging, commonly abbreviated as OPS, combines two complementary aspects of offensive performance into a single number. The formula adds on-base percentage (OBP)—which measures how frequently a player reaches base through hits, walks, or hit-by-pitch—to slugging percentage (SLUG), which measures power by accounting for extra-base hits. An OPS of .800 is generally considered solid for a major league player, while .900 represents All-Star caliber performance and 1.000 or higher indicates elite hitters.

The beauty of OPS lies in its simplicity combined with meaningful predictive value. Rather than requiring complex calculations or proprietary formulas, it can be computed from basic statistics that have been tracked for over a century. Studies consistently show that team OPS correlates strongly with runs scored, making it a reliable proxy for offensive contribution. It gained widespread mainstream adoption in the 2000s as statistical literacy increased among fans and media covering the game.

OPS is not without its imperfections. The scale of OBP and SLUG are weighted differently relative to their run value, meaning an equal improvement in each component does not contribute equally to OPS. Additionally, OPS treats all on-base events identically when walks arguably create fewer runs than singles. Despite these flaws, it remains one of the most useful single metrics for evaluating hitters at a glance, and has largely displaced batting average as the go-to measure of overall hitting ability.
