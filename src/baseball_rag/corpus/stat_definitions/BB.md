A walk, officially recorded as a base on balls, occurs when a pitcher throws four pitches outside the strike zone that the batter does not swing at. The batter is awarded first base and advances if forced by runners already occupying earlier bases. Walks have been tracked as an official statistic since the earliest days of professional baseball and represent one of the most valuable outcomes for an offense beyond hitting safely.

The strategic value of drawing walks cannot be overstated in modern baseball analytics. Each walk extends innings, forces pitchers to throw additional pitches (potentially shortening their effectiveness), moves runners into scoring position without risk, and contributes directly to run-scoring opportunities. On-base percentage heavily weights walks because they represent plate discipline and an ability to identify hittable pitches while forcing the opposition to come closer to the strike zone. Players who consistently draw high walk rates—Nick Swisher, Joey Votto, and Barry Bonds being prominent examples—create value that traditional stats like batting average completely fail to capture.

Historically, walks were somewhat undervalued in mainstream baseball culture, with aggressive hitters who swung at anything sometimes celebrated over patient plate spectators. This perception has shifted dramatically as run expectancy analysis demonstrated that a walk produces nearly the same expected runs as a single in many contexts. Barry Bonds drew 232 walks in 2004 alongside his record-shattering home run total, illustrating how combining power with patience creates an almost unhittable offensive player.
