A home run occurs when a batter hits the ball over the outfield fence or, in older parks without fences, is able to circle all four bases unchallenged due to the ball leaving the playing field. In modern baseball, home runs are recorded as automatic base hits that score the batter and any runners already on base. The statistic tracks how many home runs a player hits during a season or career, making it one of the most celebrated offensive measurements in the sport.

The dead-ball era (roughly 1900-1919) saw dramatically lower home run totals due to the composition of balls used, ballpark dimensions favoring contact hitters, and an overall strategic philosophy that valued small ball over power hitting. Babe Ruth changed the game upon arriving in New York, regularly hitting 50+ homers per season when the league leader often hovered around 10-15 just a decade earlier. By the 1920s, power hitting had become central to baseball's identity.

Today's game features home run totals that would have seemed impossible in previous generations, with players like Aaron Judge (62 HR in 2022) and Shohei Ohtani exceeding 40 homers in recent seasons. Modern training methods, optimized swing mechanics, launch angle emphasis, and ballpark designs favoring hitters all contribute to the power surge. Barry Bonds holds the MLB single-season record with 73 home runs in 2001, while Hank Aaron's career total of 755 long stood as the benchmark before being surpassed by Bonds.
