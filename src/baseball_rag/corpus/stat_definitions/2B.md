A double is recorded when a batter safely reaches second base on a hit, typically by striking the ball into the gap or down the line with enough authority to prevent fielders from making a play. Doubles are often considered the hallmark of consistent, well-rounded hitters rather than pure power specialists—they require bat control, pitch selection, and line-drive ability rather than just raw strength. The statistic tracks total two-base hits accumulated over any given span.

While home runs generate more excitement and receive more attention in award discussions, doubles reveal something important about a player's approach and stroke quality that home run totals can obscure. A player who consistently drives the ball to the gaps can accumulate 40+ doubles even without elite home run power, demonstrating their ability to hit for extra bases through skill rather than sheer force. This consistency often translates to higher batting averages since line drives fall for hits more frequently than fly balls.

The all-time doubles record belongs to Pete Rose with 746 career two-base hits, followed by Tris Speaker at 792 (though Speaker's total is recognized as official). Players like Rod Carew and Hall of Fame second basemen consistently appear near the top of historical doubles leaders because their contact-oriented approaches generated line drives that split outfielders. In modern baseball, gap power remains valuable even as home run rates have climbed, as doubles contribute to rallies without depending on the all-or-nothing outcome of a home run swing.
