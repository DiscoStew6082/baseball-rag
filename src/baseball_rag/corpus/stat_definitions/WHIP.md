Walks Plus Hits Per Innings Pitched, universally abbreviated as WHIP, quantifies how many baserunners a pitcher allows per inning. The calculation is simply (Walks + Hits) / Innings Pitched, making it easy to compute and understand at a glance. A WHIP under 1.00 indicates exceptional ability to keep runners off the bases, while anything above 1.50 generally signals struggles with command and contact allowed. The metric gained popularity in fantasy baseball circles before migrating into mainstream analysis.

WHIP directly measures a pitcher's ability to prevent batters from reaching base, which logically correlates with preventing runs since baserunners are prerequisites for most scoring. Unlike ERA, WHIP is less dependent on defensive quality behind the pitcher and more reflective of raw pitching performance. It captures both command (avoiding walks) and the ability to miss bats or induce weak contact (limiting hits), creating a useful composite view of baserunner prevention.

The statistic does have blind spots that merit consideration. Not all hits are created equal—a bloop single loads the bases just as much as a line-drive double, but they carry different implications for expected run scoring. WHIP also treats home runs identically to walks since they do not register as hits or walks once they leave the ballpark. Despite these limitations, WHIP remains a valuable quick indicator of whether a pitcher is dominating hitters and setting up favorable situations for their defense.
