A putout is credited to a defensive player for recording an out, most commonly by catching a fly ball, tagging a runner, or forcing a batter to ground into a double play. The statistic measures how many times a fielder directly completes an out during the course of games, seasons, and careers. Outfielders accumulate putouts by catching fly balls, while infielders earn them through catches on line drives, force plays at bases, and strikeouts where the catcher catches the third strike.

Putouts reveal important information about defensive range and positioning that traditional fielding percentage obscures. A center fielder with 300 putouts likely has exceptional coverage ability and reads off the bat well enough to track down balls that would drop for hits against a less capable defender. First basemen traditionally lead all positions in career putouts because of their involvement in ground ball force plays, making the metric particularly meaningful at that position when evaluating defensive contributions.

The limitation of putouts as a defensive measure is that they correlate heavily with opportunities—a player on a strong defensive team with good pitching will receive more chances to make putouts than an identical player surrounded by weaker defenders. Additionally, putouts do not capture the full scope of defensive value since range and arm strength contribute to preventing hits in ways that never result in recorded outs. Despite these constraints, putout totals remain a useful component of defensive analysis when considered alongside assists and advanced metrics like defensive runs saved.
