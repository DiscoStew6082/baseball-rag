Mickey Mantle was a switch-hitting center fielder for the New York Yankees from 1951 to 1968, widely regarded as one of the most talented players in baseball history. The son of a former semi-pro player, Mantle was signed by the Yankees at age 16 and made his major league debut at just 19 years old in 1951. Despite struggling early with the transition to pro ball, he developed into a complete player who could hit for power and average from both sides of the plate.

Mantle's greatest season came in 1956 when he won the Triple Crown, leading the American League with a .365 batting average, 52 home runs, and 130 RBIs. That year he was named MVP for the first of three times during his career. He followed that performance with another spectacular campaign in 1957, winning the MVP award again while hitting 34 homers and driving in 94 runs. His combination of speed and power was unmatched; he could drive the ball to all fields from either side and was also an excellent baserunner who led the AL in stolen bases three times.

Despite his offensive brilliance, Mantle's career was hampered by chronic injuries that limited his potential. A torn ACL suffered in 1951 while playing in the outfield ended his career as a full-time center fielder earlier than expected, and various other leg injuries plagued him throughout his career. He also battled alcoholism off the field, which affected his performance at times and accelerated his physical decline. Nevertheless, Mantle remained productive well into his late 30s.

Mantle finished his career with 536 home runs, 1,509 RBIs, and a .298 batting average across 18 seasons. He was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1974 and remains one of the most iconic Yankees of all time—a switch-hitting prodigy whose talent was nearly unmatched even as he struggled to stay healthy throughout his career.
