Hank Aaron, known as "Hammerin' Hank," was one of baseball's most consistent and durable players over his remarkable 23-season career with the Milwaukee/Atlanta Braves and Milwaukee Brewers. He made his major league debut in 1954 and quickly established himself as a complete hitter with exceptional bat control and power to all fields. Aaron compiled 755 career home runs—later surpassed only by Barry Bonds—and 2,174 RBIs, both records that stood for decades before being broken.

Aaron's pursuit of Babe Ruth's all-time home run record beginning in 1973 placed him at the center of one of baseball's most scrutinized moments. As he closed in on 714, Aaron faced relentless racist threats and hostility from fans who did not want to see a Black player break Ruth's hallowed record. Despite this tremendous pressure, Aaron handled himself with extraordinary grace and dignity, letting his performance speak for itself as he continued to hit home runs throughout the summer of 1974.

On April 8, 1974, Aaron broke Ruth's record with home run number 715 off a pitch from Los Angeles Dodgers pitcher Al Downing. The moment was historic not only for baseball but for American society, representing a breakthrough for Black athletes while acknowledging the legacy of the game's greatest player. Aaron received standing ovations throughout his career that continued long after he passed Ruth's mark, and his composure under pressure became as legendary as his achievements with the bat.

Beyond the home run record, Aaron was a 25-time All-Star selection who also excelled at hitting for average, finishing with a .305 lifetime mark and 3,771 hits—the player to first reach both 3,000 hits and 600 home runs. He was inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1982 and remained active in baseball operations with the Atlanta Braves well into his later years.
