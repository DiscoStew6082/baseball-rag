George Herman "Babe" Ruth was a legendary baseball player who transformed the sport during his 22-season career with the Boston Red Sox and New York Yankees. Originally a pitcher for the Red Sox, Ruth won 89 games and posted a stellar 2.02 ERA before being traded to the Yankees in 1919. After moving to New York, he became the most feared power hitter of his era, revolutionizing the game with his prodigious home run totals and helping the Yankees become baseball's premier franchise.

Ruth led the Yankees to seven American League pennants and four World Series championships during the 1920s, a decade now known as "The Babe Ruth Era." He set numerous records including 60 home runs in 1927—a record that stood for 34 years—and finished his career with 714 homers. His career batting average of .342 remains one of the highest in history, and he led the AL in home runs an incredible 12 times. Beyond the numbers, Ruth's charismatic personality and larger-than-life presence made him America's first true sports celebrity.

One of Ruth's most legendary moments was his "called shot" during Game 3 of the 1932 World Series at Wrigley Field, where he allegedly pointed to a spot in the outfield before hitting a home run to that exact location. While the call remains debated among historians, it epitomized Ruth's confidence and flair for dramatic performance on baseball's biggest stage. His ability to deliver in clutch moments made him a mythic figure whose legend only grew after his retirement.

Ruth passed away in 1948 but his impact on baseball remains unmatched. He shifted the sport from an era of small-ball tactics like bunting and stolen bases to a power-hitting revolution that defined subsequent generations. The "Sultan of Swat" was one of the first players inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame and continues to be cited by experts as perhaps the greatest player the game has ever seen.
